Why the 1-minute typing test is the standard

Of all the timed formats, the one-minute test is the version most schools, employers, and typing certificates use — and for good reason. Sixty seconds is long enough to smooth out the noise of a slow first word or a single stumble, but short enough to run three or four attempts in a sitting without fatigue skewing the results. It is also the format that maps most directly to the metric itself: WPM means words per minute, so a full-minute run needs no extrapolation at all. When someone asks how fast you type, the number they expect is a one-minute number.

How scoring works

Your score uses the same standardized math as every serious typing test. Because real words vary in length, a "word" is defined as five characters, including spaces:

WPM = (correct characters ÷ 5) ÷ 1 minute

Only correctly typed characters count toward your WPM, so the score reflects usable speed rather than key-mashing. Accuracy is tracked separately:

Accuracy = (correct characters ÷ total characters typed) × 100%

Every keystroke counts toward the total — including mistakes you later fix — so the test rewards getting it right the first time. For a full breakdown of what the numbers mean, see our guide to what counts as a good WPM.

60 seconds vs. shorter tests: the accuracy tradeoff

A 15-second sprint measures your burst speed: you can hold your breath, hammer out a fast phrase, and post a number that flatters you. A full minute is more honest. Over 60 seconds your rhythm, your recovery from mistakes, and even mild fatigue all show up in the result, which is why one-minute scores typically land a few WPM below short-test scores for the same typist. That is not a flaw — it is the point. The one-minute figure is much closer to the speed you can actually sustain when writing an email or taking notes. It is also more repeatable: run this test three times and your scores will cluster far more tightly than three 15-second sprints will. If you are curious why test length changes your score, our guide on how accurate online WPM tests are digs into the details.

Tips for a higher 1-minute score

  • Settle in before you sprint. The first few seconds set your rhythm — start at a controlled pace and accelerate once your fingers are moving cleanly.
  • Fix errors immediately. An uncorrected error leaves every following character misaligned; a quick backspace costs less than a cascade of misses.
  • Keep accuracy above 95%. Corrections are the biggest drain on a one-minute score. Slowing down 5% often raises your net WPM.
  • Use proper technique. Touch typing from the home row is what makes a sustained minute possible — see proper finger placement and our guide to typing faster.
  • Retest under the same conditions. Same keyboard, same posture, same time of day — that is how you know a higher number is real progress.

Frequently asked questions

Is one minute long enough to measure typing speed?
Yes. Sixty seconds is long enough to smooth out a slow first word or a single stumble, but short enough to repeat several times in a sitting. That balance is why the one-minute format became the standard for schools, job screenings, and typing certificates.
What is a good score on a 1 minute typing test?
Around 40 WPM is average for adults, 60 WPM and above is good, and 80 WPM or more is fast — provided accuracy stays in the high 90s. Professional typists often exceed 100 WPM over a full minute.
Why is my 1 minute WPM lower than my 15 second WPM?
Short tests reward a burst of speed, while a full minute exposes your sustainable pace. Over 60 seconds small hesitations, corrections, and fatigue all count, so the number is usually a few WPM lower — and a more honest estimate of your real-world speed.
