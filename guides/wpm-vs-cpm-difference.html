WPM vs CPM: What's the Difference?

Some typing tests report WPM, others report CPM, and a few throw in KPH for good measure. They sound like different skills, but they're just different units for the same thing. Here's how each one is calculated, how to convert between them, and which one you should actually care about.

The definitions

  • WPM (words per minute) — the most common typing metric. Because real words vary in length, tests don't count literal words; they use a standardized "word" of five characters, including spaces.
  • CPM (characters per minute) — the raw count of characters you type per minute, no word standardization involved.
  • KPH (keystrokes per hour) — the same idea scaled up, used mainly in data-entry job listings.

The 5-character rule that connects them

Once you know that one "word" is defined as five characters, the conversion is trivial:

WPM = CPM ÷ 5  and  CPM = WPM × 5

So a 60 WPM typist is a 300 CPM typist. Same fingers, same speed, different unit — like miles versus kilometers.

Why do some tests use CPM instead of WPM?

Three practical reasons:

  1. It sidesteps the "what is a word?" problem. The five-character convention is standard but not universal — counting characters removes any ambiguity.
  2. It works across languages. In languages where "words" are hard to delimit (or where average word length differs a lot from English), CPM is the fairer unit. Many non-English typing tests default to it.
  3. It's finer-grained. On short tests, one five-character word is a big chunk of the score. Characters give smoother numbers.

Gross vs. net: the distinction that actually matters

Here's the thing — the WPM/CPM choice changes nothing about your ranking. What genuinely changes your score is whether the test counts errors:

  • Gross WPM/CPM counts every keystroke, including mistakes.
  • Net WPM/CPM counts only characters that matched the target text.

A typist making frequent errors can have a gross score dramatically higher than their net score, in either unit. When a test (or a job posting) quotes a number, "net or gross?" is a far more important question than "WPM or CPM?". Which should you track?

For English typing, track net WPM plus accuracy. It's the unit employers recognize, the one benchmark tables use, and the one you'll see quoted everywhere from job listings to Reddit flexes. If a tool gives you CPM, just divide by five and carry on. The metric pair to watch over time is:

  • Net WPM — your usable output speed.
  • Accuracy percentage — the quality behind it. Speed gains that come with falling accuracy usually evaporate under real-world conditions.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between WPM and CPM?
WPM counts standardized five-character words per minute; CPM counts individual characters per minute. They measure the same typing — CPM is roughly WPM times five.
How do I convert CPM to WPM?
Divide by five. 300 CPM = 60 WPM. To convert WPM to CPM, multiply by five.
Is CPM more accurate than WPM?
No — they're unit conversions of the same measurement. CPM is just finer-grained and language-neutral, which is why some tests prefer it.
What is a good CPM score?
Using the standard benchmarks times five: about 200 CPM is average, 300+ is good, and 400+ is fast.
