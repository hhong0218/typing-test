What's a Good WPM for Programmers?

Developers love to argue about this one. One camp says typing speed is irrelevant because coding is thinking; the other says slow typing is a tax on every hour at the keyboard. Both are partly right. Here's a realistic answer: what's "good," why code WPM is a different animal from prose WPM, and when it's worth training.

The short answer

On a standard prose typing test, 60–80 WPM is a comfortable target for programmers. That's fast enough that transcribing a thought never interrupts the thought. Below roughly 40 WPM, typing starts to intrude on your working memory; above roughly 80, extra speed rarely changes your output as a developer, because the bottleneck moves fully to thinking, reading, and debugging.

For calibration against the general population, see what is a good WPM? — 40 is about average, so a 60–80 WPM developer is comfortably ahead of it without being a speed demon.

Why your code WPM is lower than your prose WPM

Run a prose test, then try typing actual code, and watch your number drop. That's expected, for concrete reasons:

  • Symbol density. Braces, brackets, underscores, pipes, angle brackets, and operators like => or !== live on the keyboard's periphery and often require Shift. Prose almost never touches them.
  • No predictable words. English text lets your fingers roll through familiar patterns ("the", "tion", "ing"). Identifiers like getUserByIdAsync or xs_grid_offset kill that autopilot.
  • Precision cost. A typo in prose is cosmetic; a typo in code is a syntax error or, worse, a silent bug. Careful typing is slower typing.
  • Indentation and structure. Nesting, alignment, and line breaks add non-text keystrokes that tests don't reward.

There's no universal "code WPM" benchmark, so don't hunt for one — measure yourself on both and track your own gap.

Does speed even matter? An honest accounting

Programming is mostly reading, thinking, and debugging — nobody ships more features purely by typing faster. But look at where a developer's keystrokes actually go in a week: commit messages, pull request descriptions, code review comments, standup notes, Slack, documentation, search queries, and email. That's prose, and lots of it. Slow typing taxes all of it, every day.

There's a subtler cost too: flow. When typing is automatic, your ideas land in the editor while they're still fresh. When it isn't, you're context-switching between "what do I want to say" and "where is the underscore key" — and that switch is exactly the kind of interruption that makes complex thoughts fall apart.

So the honest framing: typing speed won't make you a better engineer, but slow, error-prone typing makes the engineer you already are less effective at the margins. Getting from 35 to 65 WPM is a real quality-of-life upgrade; getting from 85 to 110 is a hobby.

How to train it (without wasting time)

  1. Get a baseline on both text types. Take a standard prose test, then a code typing test with real Python, JavaScript, or Java snippets. The gap tells you what to train.
  2. Fix accuracy before speed. Backspaces are where most "slow" typing actually goes. Our guide on improving typing accuracy covers the method.
  3. Drill your weak symbols deliberately. Most developers have three or four symbols they always miss — commonly { }, _, |, and ~. Slow, correct repetitions for a few minutes a day beat generic practice.
  4. Practice on code, not just prose. Symbol fluency only comes from typing symbols in context.
  5. Keep sessions short and daily. Ten to fifteen focused minutes daily outperforms an occasional hour. Consistency is what moves the automaticity needle.

And yes — editor autocomplete, snippets, and AI assistants reduce raw typing volume. They don't eliminate it, and none of them write your code review comments for you.

Frequently asked questions

What is a good WPM for programmers?
60–80 WPM on a standard prose test is a comfortable range — fast enough that typing never bottlenecks thinking. Code typing will be slower, and that's normal.
Does typing speed matter for coding?
At the margins, yes. Developers type large amounts of prose — reviews, commits, chat, docs — and fluent typing protects flow. Past roughly 60 WPM the returns diminish.
Why is my WPM lower when typing code?
Symbols on hard-to-reach keys, unpredictable identifiers, and the precision code demands all slow you down compared to English prose.
How can programmers practice typing?
Practice on real code snippets, drill the specific symbols you miss, prioritize accuracy, and keep sessions short and daily.
