What Is WPM and How Is It Measured?

WPM is the number every typing test reports, but few people know what it actually counts. The short version: it measures how many standardized "words" you type correctly in a minute — and the definition of a word is stranger, and simpler, than you'd guess. Here's the full breakdown.

What WPM stands for

WPM stands for words per minute. It's the universal unit for typing speed, used everywhere from school assessments to job screenings to the test on this site. When someone says they type at 70 WPM, they mean they can produce roughly 70 standardized words of correct text every minute at a sustained pace.

The catch is hidden in the word "word." In a typing test, a word is not a real word.

Why a "word" is exactly five characters

Real words vary wildly in length — a is one letter, encyclopedia is twelve. If a test counted actual words, your score would swing depending on whether the passage happened to be full of short words or long ones. That would make results impossible to compare across different texts or different tools.

To fix this, typing tests define a word as five characters, including the space that follows it. This standard has been used for decades precisely because it removes the luck of the draw. Every passage, every test, every tool uses the same yardstick, so a score of 60 WPM means the same thing no matter what you typed.

So when you type the sentence "the quick brown fox," you didn't type four words in the WPM sense — you typed 20 characters, which counts as four standardized words (20 ÷ 5).

The WPM formula

With the five-character rule in hand, the calculation is straightforward:

WPM = (correct characters ÷ 5) ÷ minutes elapsed

Walk through an example. Suppose you type 300 correct characters in exactly 60 seconds:

  • 300 characters ÷ 5 = 60 standardized words
  • 60 words ÷ 1 minute = 60 WPM

If you typed those same 300 characters in 30 seconds (half a minute), your rate would be 60 words ÷ 0.5 = 120 WPM. The formula simply scales whatever you did up to a full minute.

Gross WPM vs net WPM

There are two versions of the number, and the difference matters:

Gross WPM

Gross WPM counts every character you typed, mistakes included. It measures raw finger speed but rewards you for typing fast even when you're wrong, which isn't very useful.

Net WPM

Net WPM counts only the characters you got right. It effectively subtracts the cost of your errors, so it reflects how much correct, usable text you actually produced. This is the number worth caring about, and it's what a good test reports. On this site, your WPM counts only correctly typed characters — so what you see is net speed, not flattering raw speed.

How accuracy is measured alongside WPM

Speed never travels alone. The companion metric is accuracy, the share of your keystrokes that matched the target text:

Accuracy = (correct characters ÷ total characters typed) × 100%

Every character you enter counts toward the total, including ones you later fix, so accuracy rewards getting it right the first time. Because net WPM already discards wrong characters, your speed and accuracy scores move together: as accuracy climbs, more of your keystrokes "count," and your net WPM rises even if your hands never move faster. What the number means once you have it

For rough context: the average adult types around 40 WPM, a proficient typist sits in the 65 to 80 WPM range, and professional typists often exceed 100 WPM. But the "right" number depends entirely on what you're doing — 50 to 60 WPM with high accuracy handles most office and data-entry work comfortably. Frequently asked questions

What does WPM stand for?
WPM stands for words per minute. It's the standard unit for typing speed and measures how many standardized words you can type correctly in one minute, where one word is defined as five characters including spaces.
Why is a word counted as five characters?
Real words range from one to many letters, so counting actual words would make speed depend on which words appear in the text. Fixing a word at five characters, including the space, gives every test the same scale and makes scores comparable across passages and tools.
How is WPM calculated?
Divide the number of correctly typed characters by five to get standardized words, then divide by the minutes elapsed. For example, 300 correct characters in 60 seconds is 60 words over one minute, which equals 60 WPM. Most tests count only correct characters, so the result reflects usable net speed.
What is the difference between gross and net WPM?
Gross WPM counts every character you typed, including mistakes. Net WPM counts only correct characters, subtracting the cost of errors. Net WPM is more meaningful because it reflects how much correct text you actually produced, not just how fast your fingers moved.
