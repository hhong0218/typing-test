How to Type Faster: 8 Proven Tips

Typing speed is a learnable skill, not a talent. With the right technique and a few minutes of daily practice, almost anyone can climb from 40 words per minute to 70, 80, or beyond. Here is how to do it.

1. Learn proper touch typing

The single biggest jump in typing speed comes from switching to touch typing — typing without looking at your hands. Instead of hunting for each key, your fingers learn fixed positions and reach for keys automatically. It feels awkward at first and may even slow you down for a few days, but it is the foundation everything else builds on. Our touch typing guide for beginners walks through the first steps.

2. Anchor on the home row

Rest your left fingers on ASDF and your right fingers on JKL;. The small bumps on the F and J keys let you find this position without looking. Every other key is reached from here and your fingers return to home after each stroke. A consistent home-row anchor is what lets you type by feel.

3. Prioritize accuracy over raw speed

It is tempting to mash keys as fast as possible, but every mistake costs you a backspace and breaks your flow. Slowing down by 10 percent to hit 98 percent accuracy usually produces a higher net speed than racing at 90 percent. Build the habit of clean, correct strokes and let speed catch up.

4. Keep your eyes on the screen

Glancing at the keyboard is the habit that holds most people back. Force yourself to read the text on screen and trust your fingers. If you cover your hands for the first week, the discomfort fades fast and your muscle memory locks in.

5. Practice in short, regular sessions

Ten focused minutes a day beats a two-hour cram once a week. Typing speed is built on muscle memory, and muscle memory responds to frequency. Make it a daily ritual — a quick warm-up before work is plenty.

6. Drill your weak keys and common words

Most typists have a handful of letters or combinations they fumble repeatedly. Notice your error patterns and drill them deliberately. Practicing the most common English words — the, and, that, with, your — also pays off, because they make up a huge share of real text.

7. Fix your posture and setup

Sit upright, keep your wrists floating rather than resting hard on the desk, and position the keyboard so your forearms are roughly level. A comfortable, repeatable setup reduces fatigue and lets you practice longer without strain.

8. Measure your progress

You improve what you measure. Take a short timed test regularly so you can see your WPM trend upward and spot when accuracy slips. Tracking turns vague effort into visible progress — and a little competition with your past self is great motivation.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to type faster?
Most people see a noticeable improvement within two to four weeks of short daily practice. Touch typing can feel slower for the first few days before your speed surpasses your old habits.
Should I focus on speed or accuracy first?
Accuracy first. Errors force backspacing and break your rhythm, so high accuracy almost always produces a higher net WPM. Speed follows once accuracy is consistently above 95 percent.
Does looking at the keyboard slow me down?
Yes. Glancing down breaks your flow and prevents muscle memory from forming. Keeping your eyes on the screen is one of the biggest single improvements you can make.
