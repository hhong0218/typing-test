Typing Practice for Kids: A Parent's Guide

Typing is one of the most useful skills a child can pick up early — it pays off in schoolwork, creativity, and confidence for years. The trick is to keep expectations realistic and the practice genuinely fun. Here's how to help a kid learn to type without it ever feeling like a chore.

When should kids start?

Most children are ready to begin around ages 6 or 7, when they can comfortably reach across a keyboard and have started reading. Before that, there's no rush — simply letting a young child press keys, find letters in their name, and play with words builds familiarity. Formal touch typing tends to click best between ages 8 and 11, when hands are a little bigger and attention spans are longer.

Every child develops at their own pace, so treat age ranges as loose guidance rather than a deadline. A motivated seven-year-old and a reluctant ten-year-old can both thrive with the right approach.

Realistic WPM goals by age

Kids' typing speeds vary widely, and that's completely normal. These ranges are a friendly reference, not a target to stress over — accuracy and good habits matter far more than the number.

AgeTypical range
6–85–15 WPM
9–1115–25 WPM
12–1425–40 WPM
15+35–50+ WPM

Notice the wide bands — a child at the lower end isn't behind, and one at the upper end isn't a prodigy. What you really want to see over time is steady improvement and clean, accurate typing.

Make it fun, not a drill

The fastest way to kill a child's interest is to make typing feel like homework. The fastest way to build it is to make it play. A few ideas that work well:

  • Let them type about what they love. A passage about dinosaurs, soccer, or a favorite game holds attention far better than generic practice text.
  • Set tiny personal-best challenges. "Can you beat your score from yesterday by one?" is more motivating than chasing a distant goal.
  • Celebrate accuracy and effort, not just speed. Praise a clean run with no mistakes as loudly as a fast one.
  • Keep sessions short. Ending while they still want more is the secret to them coming back tomorrow.

Use games and gamification

Children learn beautifully through games, and typing is no exception. Typing games that use points, levels, badges, and friendly characters turn repetitive key drills into something kids actually ask to do. The game loop — try, get a score, try to beat it — naturally encourages the repetition that builds muscle memory.

You can also build your own light gamification at home: a sticker chart for practice days, a small reward for reaching a streak, or a weekly "race" against a parent (with a generous handicap). The goal is to make progress visible and celebrated.

Build good habits early

The habits a child forms first tend to stick, so it's worth gently steering them toward good technique from the start:

  • Home row first. Teach the resting position — left fingers on ASDF, right fingers on JKL; — and the little bumps on F and J that help find it without looking.
  • Eyes on the screen. Encourage looking at the text rather than the keyboard, even if it's slower at first.
  • All ten fingers. Discourage two-finger hunt-and-peck before it becomes a habit that's hard to undo later.
  • Comfortable seat. Make sure the chair and keyboard are at a height where small arms and wrists sit naturally.

Keep screen time balanced

Typing practice is screen time, so fold it into your family's overall balance rather than treating it as extra. Short, purposeful sessions — a few minutes a day — fit easily and don't crowd out play, reading, or time outdoors. Build in regular breaks to stand up and stretch, and try to keep practice as a positive, low-pressure part of the day. If you have any specific concerns about your child's screen use, your pediatrician is the right person to ask.

Frequently asked questions

At what age should kids start learning to type?
Around ages 6 to 7 is a common starting point, once children can reach across a keyboard and have begun reading. Formal touch typing usually clicks best between ages 8 and 11, when hands are bigger and attention spans are longer.
What is a good typing speed for a child?
It varies a lot. As a rough guide, many kids reach about 15 WPM in early elementary, 20 to 30 WPM by ages 10 to 12, and 30 to 45 WPM by their early teens. Accuracy and good habits matter more than a specific number.
How long should a child practice typing each day?
Five to ten minutes a day, a few days a week, works best. Younger children do well with even shorter bursts. Short and frequent builds muscle memory without burnout.
How do I make typing practice fun for kids?
Turn it into a game — use typing games with points and levels, set small personal-best challenges, let them type about topics they love, and celebrate progress rather than raw speed.
