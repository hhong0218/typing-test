7 Common Typing Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Most slow typists aren't lacking talent — they're stuck behind a few specific bad habits. The good news is that every one of these mistakes has a clear fix, and correcting even one or two can add real words per minute. Here are the seven that hold people back most.

1. Looking at the keyboard

This is the big one. When you glance down to find each key, you cap your speed at how fast your eyes can hunt — and you never give your fingers the chance to memorize where keys live. You also catch errors late, because your attention is on your hands instead of the text appearing on screen.

The fix: keep your eyes on the screen and trust your fingers. For the first week, cover your hands with a cloth or sit in the dark if you have to. The discomfort fades within days, and once it does your muscle memory locks in and your speed climbs.

2. Hunt-and-peck with two fingers

Typing with just your index fingers — or a chaotic mix of two or three — feels fine at first but has a hard ceiling around 30 to 40 WPM. Each keystroke requires a search, and your hands are constantly traveling across the board instead of staying anchored.

The fix: commit to touch typing with all ten fingers. Start by learning the home row (ASDF for the left hand, JKL; for the right) and which finger owns which key. It will feel slower for a few days, then it overtakes hunt-and-peck and keeps climbing. See our finger placement guide for exactly which finger hits what.

3. Using the wrong fingers

Even people who "touch type" often use a personal, inconsistent fingering — stretching the wrong finger to a key or always hitting the spacebar with the same thumb in an awkward way. Inconsistent fingering means your hand has to reset its position constantly, which slows you down and breeds errors.

The fix: learn the standard finger-to-key assignments and stick to them, even when an old habit feels faster. Consistency is what lets your hands move on autopilot. Slow, correct repetition rewires the habit within a couple of weeks.

4. Rushing past your control speed

Trying to type faster than you can accurately is self-defeating. Every mistake costs a backspace and a retype, so racing at 90 percent accuracy is usually slower in net terms than a controlled pace at 98 percent.

The fix: deliberately slow down to about 75 percent of your top speed until your accuracy sits above 97 percent. Clean technique built at a controlled pace becomes permanent speed. Our accuracy guide covers this slow-to-go-fast method in depth.

5. Not returning to the home row

A subtle but costly habit: letting your fingers drift after each word instead of returning them to the home row. Once your hands lose their anchor, every reach becomes a guess and accuracy collapses.

The fix: use the bumps on the F and J keys to re-find home without looking, and consciously return your fingers there between bursts. After enough practice the reset becomes automatic.

6. Poor posture and setup

Slouching, resting your wrists hard on the desk, and a keyboard placed too high or too far away all create tension and fatigue. Tired, tense hands make more mistakes and tire out faster, so your practice sessions get shorter and less productive.

The fix: sit upright with your feet flat, shoulders relaxed, and the keyboard positioned so your forearms are roughly level with the floor. Keep your wrists floating rather than pressed down. A comfortable setup lets you practice longer without strain.

7. Practicing inconsistently

Many people do one long, frustrated typing session, see little progress, and give up. Typing is built on muscle memory, and muscle memory responds to frequency, not heroic single efforts.

The fix: practice in short, regular sessions — ten to fifteen focused minutes a day beats a two-hour cram once a week. Make it a small daily ritual and track your numbers so you can see the trend.

Putting it together

You don't have to fix all seven at once. Pick the one or two that describe you best — usually looking at the keyboard and rushing are the heavy hitters — and focus there first. As each old habit fades, the next one becomes easier to address, and your speed and accuracy rise together.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common typing mistake?
Looking at the keyboard while you type. It breaks the connection between your eyes and the screen, prevents muscle memory from forming, and caps your speed at the rate you can visually hunt for keys.
Is hunt-and-peck typing bad?
It works for short bursts but has a low ceiling, usually around 30 to 40 WPM, because you spend most of your effort searching for keys. Switching to touch typing with all ten fingers roughly doubles most people's long-term speed.
Why do I make more mistakes when I type fast?
Because you're typing past your control speed. Slow down to about 75 percent of your top speed until accuracy is consistently above 97 percent, then let speed rebuild on a clean foundation.
Does posture affect typing speed?
Yes. Slouching, locked wrists, and a poorly positioned keyboard cause fatigue and tension that hurt accuracy and endurance. Sit upright with relaxed shoulders, floating wrists, and forearms roughly level.
