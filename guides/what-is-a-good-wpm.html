What Is a Good WPM?

"WPM" gets thrown around constantly, but what number actually counts as good? The short answer: about 40 is average, 60-plus is good, and 80-plus is fast. Here is what those numbers mean, how they're measured, and what you really need for work and study.

How WPM is measured

WPM stands for words per minute. Because real words vary in length, typing tests use a standardized "word" of five characters, including spaces. So the formula is:

WPM = (correct characters ÷ 5) ÷ minutes elapsed

Good tests count only the characters you typed correctly. That means WPM reflects your usable speed, not how fast you can mash keys and rack up errors you'd have to fix.

For context, the average adult types around 40 WPM, a proficient typist sits in the 65–80 range, and professional typists often top 100 WPM.

Why accuracy matters as much as speed

A high raw WPM is meaningless if half of it is wrong. Accuracy — the share of keystrokes that matched the target text — directly affects your net speed, because every mistake costs a backspace and a break in rhythm. A typist at 60 WPM with 98 percent accuracy is more productive than one hitting 75 WPM at 88 percent. Aim for accuracy in the high 90s, and let speed grow from there.

What WPM do you need?

  • Everyday computer use: 40 WPM is comfortable for email, messaging, and browsing.
  • Office and data-entry jobs: employers often look for 50–60 WPM with strong accuracy.
  • Writing and programming: 60–80 WPM keeps your typing from being the bottleneck on your thinking.
  • Transcription and court reporting: these specialized roles demand far higher speeds, often well above 100 WPM.

How to improve your number

Whatever your current WPM, you can raise it. The fundamentals are touch typing, accuracy-first practice, and short daily sessions. Frequently asked questions

What is a good WPM?
Around 40 WPM is average. Above 60 WPM is good, and 80 WPM or more is fast. Professional typists often exceed 100 WPM.
How is WPM calculated?
Tests use a standardized five-character word. WPM equals correct characters divided by five, scaled to one minute. Counting only correct characters reflects usable speed.
What WPM do I need for a typing job?
Many office and data-entry roles look for 50 to 60 WPM with high accuracy. Specialized roles like transcription require much higher speeds.
